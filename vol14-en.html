Last month, we explored the idea that the system is often at the heart of workplace issues. Yet, even within the same system, every team has a different pulse. Why? In many cases, a leader’s judgment and approach act as the final filter for how a team functions. Blake and Mouton described leadership through two key dimensions: concern for people and concern for results. Daniel Goleman also emphasized that a leader’s behavior shapes a team’s climate and performance. These perspectives remind us that every leader operates with their own standards and leadership style. Our leadership style is an accumulation of our past experiences and decisions. It becomes a natural framework that guides how we interpret situations and make choices. As a result, we often rely on familiar ways of thinking and leading.  However, this familiarity can sometimes create leadership blind spots.

When a certain approach repeatedly produces good results, it can gradually become our personal “right answer.” As a result, we may continue using the same approach even in situations that require a different perspective. For example, an emphasis on autonomy could inadvertently lead to a lack of support, while a cautious approach might result in over-analyzing and stalling progress.
The goal is not to change your leadership style entirely. Instead, it is to recognize the areas we may have overlooked and make conscious efforts to strengthen them. These small efforts can broaden a leader’s perspective and support more balanced decision-making. Take a few minutes to check your style and see where you might want to stretch your perspective.
